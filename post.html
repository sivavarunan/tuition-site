how to actually prepare for O/L maths
Cramming the week before the exam is a very common plan. It's also a very stressful one. Here's an alternative — slower, calmer, and far more effective in practice.

Start from the syllabus, not the textbook

The syllabus is the contract between the examiner and you. Read it once, then turn each subtopic into a single-line question you should be able to answer. By the end you'll have a checklist of perhaps eighty short prompts. That checklist is your study plan — far more useful than "do all the past papers."

Past papers, but slowly

Past papers are gold, but only if you actually understand each step. Don't time yourself on the first three or four. Sit with each question, and when you get something wrong, write the reason in your own words next to it. Patterns emerge fast — most students discover that the same two or three concepts cause most of their lost marks.

Trust the boring stuff

Show your working. Label your axes. Re-read the question before you answer. These are unglamorous habits that win marks every single paper. Build them in low-stakes sessions and they'll be automatic on exam day.
